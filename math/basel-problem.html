The Basel Problem

June 1, 2025

The Basel problem asks for the precise sum of the reciprocals of the squares of the natural numbers:

$$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2} = \frac{1}{1^2} + \frac{1}{2^2} + \frac{1}{3^2} + \cdots$$

This sum had stumped mathematicians for nearly a century before Euler solved it in 1734, showing the answer is $\frac{\pi^2}{6}$. His proof is a beautiful piece of mathematical audacity.

Euler's Approach

Euler's key insight was to treat $\sin(x)$ as an infinite polynomial whose roots are $x = 0, \pm\pi, \pm 2\pi, \ldots$, and then compare the coefficient of $x^2$ with the expansion of $\sin(x)/x$:

$$\frac{\sin x}{x} = 1 - \frac{x^2}{6} + \frac{x^4}{120} - \cdots$$

Thinking of it as a product over its roots:

$$\frac{\sin x}{x} = \prod_{n=1}^{\infty} \left(1 - \frac{x^2}{n^2\pi^2}\right)$$

Expanding and matching the coefficient of $x^2$ on both sides gives:

$$-\frac{1}{6} = -\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2 \pi^2}$$

Therefore:

$$\boxed{\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2} = \frac{\pi^2}{6}}$$

Euler's argument wasn't fully rigorous by modern standards — the product formula for sine requires justification — but the result is correct and the proof was revolutionary.
